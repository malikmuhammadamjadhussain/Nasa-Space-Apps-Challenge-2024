Greenhouse Gas Emissions from Industries

CBO estimates that the manufacturing sector was responsible for 12 percent of U.S. greenhouse gas emissions in 2021. About 75 percent of those emissions came from burning fuel to create heat, and the rest were by-products of industrial processes that transform materials into products.

GHG in Industries

Mitigation strategies for industries include switching to cleaner energy sources (e.g., renewable energy), improving energy efficiency, adopting carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies, and reducing waste emissions through better management techniques.

Energy Consumption:

Industrial processes, especially in sectors like cement, steel, aluminum, and chemicals, require a lot of energy, generated from burning fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas. This releases large amounts of carbon dioxide, the most prevalent greenhouse gas.

Chemical Reactions:

Certain industrial processes emit GHGs as byproducts. For instance, cement production releases CO₂ during the calcination of limestone, and the production of chemicals like ammonia and nitric acid results in nitrous oxide (N₂O), a potent greenhouse gas.

Fugitive Emissions:

Industries such as oil and gas operations can release methane (CH₄), another potent greenhouse gas, through leaks during extraction, transport, and storage.

Industrial Waste:

Industrial waste management, particularly the decomposition of organic waste, can produce methane emissions. Wastewater treatment can also release methane and nitrous oxide.

Other GHGs:

Some industrial processes use fluorinated gases (like HFCs, PFCs, SF₆), which are much more potent than CO₂, though released in smaller amounts.
